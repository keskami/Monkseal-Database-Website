About the Species

The Hawaiian monk seal is one of the most endangered seal species in the world. These seals are endemic to the Hawaiian Archipelago, which means that they only live here, and nowhere else in the world. Hawaiian monk seals are protected under the federal Endangered Species Act, the Marine Mammal Protection Act, and Hawaii State law.

After nearly being wiped out by exploitation and disturbance in the 1800s,the Hawaiian monk seal population rebounded slightly, only to decline again steeply in the late 1950s. This trend continued into the last decade, with the current population numbers at only about one-third of historic population levels: around 1,400 seals in total— with about ~1,100 seals in the remote Northwestern Hawaiian Islands (NWHI) and only about ~300 seals spread throughout the main Hawaiian Islands (MHI). Thanks to the conservation and recovery efforts of NOAA and their partners, decades of decline have been slowly reversing, and the population has been growing at an average rate of about 2 percent per year since 2013. This is great news! However, it is only the beginning of a long road to population recovery, and the Hawaiian monk seals still need your help.

The Hawaiian monk seal is one of NOAA Fisheries' Species in the Spotlight, a concerted effort to highlight the oceans most endangered species and raise awareness of ongoing conservation efforts.

Appearance

Newborn monk seal pups are born all black, but they typically shed this black coat prior to weaning. Weaned pups and older seals are dark gray to brown on their back and light gray to yellowish brown on their belly.

Some seals are born with “natural bleach” marks, or small areas of skin and fur that lack pigment. Sort of like a birthmark, these natural bleaches are unique and can be used to help identify which seal you are looking at.

It’s a big scary ocean, and it can be a hard life out there for a seal. As seals age, they collect scars from a variety of injuries: large shark bites, cookie cutter shark bites, entanglements, boat interactions, etc. If the scars are deep enough to last through their annual molt, and are unique enough in shape and location, then they can also be used to help identify a seal.

Monk seals undergo a "catastrophic molt" about once per year, where they shed the entire top layer of their skin and all of their fur. If you aren’t expecting it, sometimes it can look like the seal is in distress or has been injured, best rest assured they are just fine although the process can be very itchy and uncomfortable, and the seals can be a little extra sensitive and grumpy during this time. So while molting is a normal part of life for a seal, it’s best to give them extra space.

Monk seals with a bright and silvery coat have just molted. Their fresh new skin and fur is extra shiny, but the molting process can take a lot out of a seal. They might need to go foraging for some fishy snacks.

Is that a green seal? Seals that have spent a long time foraging at sea can grow algae on their fur. This is common around the eyes, mouth, flippers, and belly. And “being green” is especially common in juvenile seals; it can sometimes look like they are wearing a green mask. Seals that look green haven't molted recently and may be getting ready to shed into a new silvery coat.

Male and female monk seals are very similar in size and shape. You can distinguish males from females by looking at their belly. Both male and female seals have a 5 point mark on their bellies: the teats are 4 points forming a square, with the 5th point being the umbilicus (belly button) in the center of that square. In males, the penile groove is posterior to the umbilicus, and extends forward in a ridge from the pelvis.

Behavior & Diet

Monk seals spend two-thirds of their time foraging at sea and use coral beds as foraging habitat. Hawaiian monk seals are "generalist" feeders, which means they eat a wide variety of foods depending on what's available. They eat many types of common fishes, squids, octopuses, eels, and crustaceans (crabs, shrimps, and lobsters). They tend not to target most of the popular gamefish species, such as ulua (giant trevally), pāpio (baby ulua), or the ‘ō‘io (bonefish). Instead, they usually prefer to target prey that hides in the sand, under rocks, and in pockets in the coral.

Monk seals haul-out on land to rest, molt, give birth, and nurse their pups on the sand, corals, and volcanic rock shorelines. They prefer sandy, protected beaches surrounded by shallow waters for pupping. Seals may look like they are just being lazy on the beach, but often they are recovering from long, multi-day, open ocean swims to far-off reefs or between islands. They need their rest to recuperate, so please respect their distance and adhere to the Seal Resting Area closures.

Hawaiian monk seals can hold their breath for up to 20 minutes, and dive more than 1,800 feet below the surface. However, they usually don’t show off quite so much: the average dive for a monk seal is about 6 minutes long and to depths of less than 200 feet, where they forage on the seafloor.

Hawaiian monk seals are mostly solitary and don't live in colonies like other seals. Monk seals appreciate their personal space, but they do sometimes lie near each other in pairs or small groups, and males will sometimes try to “attend" a female by laying near her while she rests. They usually sleep on beaches, sometimes for days at a time and also occasionally sleep in tidepools and small underwater caves.

Hawaiian monk seals do not migrate seasonally, but can travel hundreds of miles in the open ocean. Individual seals often frequent the same beaches over and over, but they do not defend territories.

Lifespan & Reproduction

Monk seals can live to over 30 years of age, but few actually live that long. Unfortunately, survival rates can be very low for juvenile seals (under 4 years old). It is a hard life out there for a little seal. But survival rates do get better once the seals mature; they grow, they learn how to successfully forage, avoid many dangerous situations, and get big enough to look less tasty to other marine predators. The average lifespan for an adult Hawaiian monk seal is around ~25 years.

Monk seals mate at sea, and the event is rarely witnessed. The youngest documented female to give birth was 4 years old, but typically females in the main Hawaiian Islands begin reproducing at about 5 to 6 years old. Monk seals don’t usually begin reproducing until about 7 to 10 years old in the Northwestern Hawaiian Islands, where resources can be more scarce.

Monk seal females typically give birth to pups on sandy, protected beaches surrounded by shallow waters. Mothers and pups will remain on this natal beach for about 6 weeks. The majority of that time will be spent nursing, growing, and resting, but there will also be some play and swimming lessons in later weeks as the pups get bigger and stronger. Mothers want to make sure that their pups get as big and fat as she can make them before weaning, transferring up to one-third of their body weight to the pup in six weeks. But mothers do not leave their pups to find food, so after nursing for so long, they are emaciated and must go back to sea to forage. Mothers leave their pups abruptly, occasionally returning once or twice within the first few hours, and the pup will be weaned. Weaned pups will remain near their natal beach, exploring, learning, and living off the fat their mothers gave them, until they too feel the need to go further out for food.
