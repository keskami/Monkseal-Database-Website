Intro

The ocean is a vibrant ecosystem, home to countless marine organisms. It can also be a source of deep appreciation and delightful recreation for humans. We can do our part to co-exist peacefully so all creatures can enjoy the ocean!

Interaction Guidelines

Nearshore fishing (shorecasting / spearfishing)

  • If monk seals are near, reel in your line, bring in your catch near you, and take a short break or change locations.
  • Use barbless circle hooks.
  • Reuse or share your leftover bait—don’t feed seals.
  • Clean catch away from seals.
  • Accidents happen! Never approach a hooked or entangled seal—they are wild animals. Call for help: (888) 256-9840.
  • Never try to reel in a seal that may be hooked. If the hook is set, carefully take in any slack line and then cut the line as close to the seal as safely possible.
  • If the hook is not visible in or around the seal’s mouth or throat, there is a possibility that the hook has been ingested, and pulling on the line may cause serious injury or death.
  • If the hook is not set, put slack on the line and give the seal time to release itself.
  • (Source: Fishing with Aloha Brochure.pdf)
  • Register and monitor your gillnets.

Surfing / SUP / board sports
Swimming / snorkeling / diving
Jet skis / boating

Go slow and keep an eye out when seals are in the area. Seals can dive for up to 20 minutes, but they do need to surface to get air. You don’t want to strike a seal with your boat, or propeller, and they don’t want that either. Blunt force trauma and boat strikes are a significant source of mortality among monk seals in the main Hawaiian Islands.

Remember that monk seals approaching the shore are often just returning from long foraging trips at sea. They are coming on land to rest and recuperate, please let them come ashore unmolested.

Seals can sometimes become very inquisitive with boaters, boarders, swimmers, and divers. If a seal is in the water, calmly come back to shore, or get back in your boat, and appreciate the animal from a respectful distance (at least 50 ft.)

Do not approach monk seals in the water. They are wild animals, and if they become too habituated to humans, they may become a danger to both themselves and to human safety.

Never chase or harass a Hawaiian monk seal! Not only is it stressful and dangerous for the animal, it is also a violation of state and federal law.

Never approach a mother seal while she is in the water with her pup!!! Seal pups are very vulnerable to predation, and seal mothers can be extremely protective of their pups. Adult female seals can weigh several hundred pounds, have very strong jaws, and are evolutionarily adapted to defend their pups from sharks. They can also definitely out-swim you. Just don’t do it, for your own safety, and the safety of the seals.

You should always be careful around monk seals, but sometimes, accidents happen. For help, or to report monk seal sightings or encounters, please call the NOAA hotline: (888) 256-9840.
